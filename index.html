Abstract

Recent advances in humanoid whole-body motion tracking enable robots to reproduce human-like poses by following reference keypoint trajectories. However, keypoint tracking alone is insufficient for object interaction tasks such as sitting on a chair, wiping a board, or pushing furniture, where the robot can reach the correct pose without ever making meaningful physical contact with the object. We present ContactMimic, a contact-conditioned imitation learning framework that augments keypoint tracking with explicit binary contact labels as a conditioning signal. Our key insight is to train a single policy on paired motion data (a default trajectory with intended contact and an augmented counterpart where target contacts are suppressed via collision-geometry inflation), combined with train-time contact-label augmentations. The resulting policy decouples contact behavior from keypoint geometry, enabling it to both achieve precise physical contact and controllably produce or suppress contact by switching the conditioning label at evaluation time. We demonstrate controllable contact in simulation across 11 diverse human-object interaction motions, and validate sim-to-real transfer on the Unitree G1 robot across 5 motions.

Method Overview

Our pipeline turns a human-object interaction motion capture clip into a contact-aware humanoid policy in three stages:

  1. Retargeting. A motion clip is retargeted to the G1 humanoid, producing a reference keypoint trajectory and per-body reference contact labels against the object.
  2. Paired motion generation. For each clip we synthesize an augmented counterpart by inflating the collision geometry of the target object part(s), yielding two trajectories with similar keypoint structure but opposite target contact labels.
  3. Contact-aware RL training. A single contact-conditioned policy is trained on the paired trajectories, supervised by keypoint-tracking rewards plus a contact-label matching reward and a contact-distance reward.

Results

Contact controllability

The same policy produces or suppresses contact depending on the conditioning label, even when the keypoint reference is held fixed.

Real-world deployment

Label-following behavior transfers to a Unitree G1 humanoid robot across five motions.

Necessity of contact conditioning

A keypoint-only baseline (BeyondMimic) tracks pose accurately but fails to establish meaningful physical contact, validating that keypoint tracking alone is an incomplete task specification.
